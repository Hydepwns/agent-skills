# Paul Graham — startup era (2005-2015)

Source: https://www.paulgraham.com (essays dated 2005-01 through 2015-11, spanning the founding of Y Combinator through the post-unicorn YC era)
Studied from: all 135 essays from this period — including "How to Start a Startup", "How to Do What You Love", "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule", "Do Things that Don't Scale", "How to Get Startup Ideas", "Startup = Growth", "Schlep Blindness", "Ramen Profitable", "The Fatal Pinch", "Black Swan Farming", "Be Good", "Cities and Ambition", "Hackers' Guide to Investors", "How to Disagree", "Lies We Tell Kids", "Default Alive or Default Dead?", "Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas", "Writing and Speaking", "Keep Your Identity Small", "How to Convince Investors", "How to Raise Money", "The Top Idea in Your Mind", and 113 others.

## The core move

Startup-era PG writes in the voice of someone who has watched the same mistake happen hundreds of times and is now going to name it for you so you can stop. Each essay opens with a flat thesis sentence (no anecdote, no setup), runs an analogy from physics or sports or kitchen work to make the abstract claim feel mechanical, and lands on a portable imperative — usually two to four words — that you could write on a wall.

## Sentence rhythm

- Claim-first opening sentences with no warm-up. "A startup is a company designed to grow fast." [from growth.txt]. "If you have a US startup called X and you don't have x.com, you should probably change your name." [from name.txt]. "Fundraising is brutal." [from fundraising.txt]
- Short declarative thunderclaps after analytic stretches. "The only essential thing is growth." [from growth.txt]. "That last sentence is the fatal one." [from pinch.txt]. "Be relentlessly resourceful." [from relres.txt].
- Sentences chained by leading "But," "So," "And," "Which," "Or" — paratactic instead of complex. "Which means you can use growth like a compass to make almost every decision you face." [from growth.txt]
- Quasi-mathematical formulations dropped raw into prose. "Microsoft : Google :: Google : Facebook." [from ambitious.txt]. "In the Valley, lightning has a sign bit." [from hubs.txt].
- Triadic enumerations escalating to a verdict. "Don't ignore your dreams; don't work too much; say what you think; cultivate friendships; be happy." [from todo.txt]

## Paragraph structure

- The thesis sentence sits at the top, the rest of the paragraph defends it. Paragraphs rarely build to a conclusion; the conclusion opens.
- Paragraphs are short — typically 2-5 sentences. Pivotal moments are isolated as one- or two-sentence paragraphs that carry disproportionate weight.
- Section headers are one or two words, almost always nouns: "Growth", "Compass", "Value", "Deals", "Schleps", "Forces", "Market". The header compresses the section to a noun and the prose unpacks it.
- Footnotes do the work parentheticals did in the early era — caveats, anecdotes, and second-order qualifications all migrate downstairs so the body stays declarative. Long essays carry 10-27 numbered notes.
- Closings collapse the essay to a single transferable imperative or aphorism. "Live in the future and build what's missing." [from startupideas.txt]. "Just learn." [from before.txt].

## Rhetorical moves

1. **Coined-term-as-handle**: name a phenomenon in scare quotes, then use the name as load-bearing shorthand. "I'm going to call the situation I described in the first paragraph 'the fatal pinch.' I try to resist coining phrases, but making up a name for this situation may snap founders into realizing when they're in it." [from pinch.txt]. Also: "ramen profitable" [from ramenprofitable.txt], "schlep blindness" [from schlep.txt], "default alive or default dead" [from aord.txt].
2. **Concrete-domain analogy carrying the whole argument**: a single physical activity (lifting, skiing, drinking through a straw) becomes the lens for the abstract claim. "When people hurt themselves lifting heavy things, it's usually because they try to lift with their back. The right way to lift heavy things is to let your legs do the work. Inexperienced founders make the same mistake when trying to convince investors." [from convince.txt]
3. **Math-as-proof**: a quick numerical demonstration converts an intuition into an inevitability. "Suppose your company is making $1000 a month now, and you've made something so great that it's growing at 5% a week. Two years from now, you'll be making about $160k a month." [from safe.txt]
4. **Concrete famous-case as exhibit**: a specific named startup carries the principle. "The most striking example I know of schlep blindness is Stripe, or rather Stripe's idea." [from schlep.txt]. "Steve Wozniak's problem was that he wanted his own computer." [from growth.txt].
5. **The way-to-X-is-not-to-Y inversion**: state the conventional path, flip it. "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself." [from startupideas.txt]
6. **Definition-by-distinction**: open by ruling out what the term doesn't mean, then declare what it does. "Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary for a startup to work on technology, or take venture funding, or have some sort of 'exit.' The only essential thing is growth." [from growth.txt]
7. **CS metaphor smuggled into life advice**: greedy algorithms, breadth-first search, compiled programs, exception handling. "Your mind is like a compiled program you've lost the source of." [from know.txt]. "For someone on the maker's schedule, having a meeting is like throwing an exception." [from makersschedule.txt].
8. **Second-person imperative close**: end on a bare command addressed to the reader. "Just learn." [from before.txt]. "Be relentlessly resourceful." [from relres.txt]. "Make something people want."

## Emotional register

- Authoritative, plainspoken, declarative. The voice has watched this play out and knows what's coming. It does not ask permission.
- Hedges ("I think," "probably," "almost," "in fact") arrive in pairs around big claims — the qualifiers come from confidence, not uncertainty. He's not asking, he's narrowing.
- Self-deprecating asides about his own past mistakes. "I made it myself. In 1995 I started a company to put art galleries online. But galleries didn't want to be online." [from startupideas.txt]. The confession licenses the lesson.
- Humor is dry, often arrives as a single mild swear or a cocked metaphor — never performative, always in service of puncturing pretension.

## What to borrow

1. **Open with the thesis sentence. No preamble.** "A startup is a company designed to grow fast." Then defend it. The flat opener trains the reader to expect substance, not setup.
2. **Run a physical-domain analogy through the argument** instead of decorating with one. Pick the analogy that matches the mechanic (lifting for fundraising, skiing for instinct, throwing an exception for interruption) and let it carry the explanation.
3. **Show the math.** When a claim depends on growth, compounding, or proportion, write the actual numbers in the sentence. "Two years from now, you'll be making about $160k a month." The number does the persuading.
4. **Name the phenomenon you're describing**, in quotes, the first time. Then use the name. This converts a three-paragraph argument into a one-phrase handle the reader can deploy elsewhere.
5. **End on a bare two-to-four-word imperative.** "Just learn." "Be relentlessly resourceful." "Make something people want." The imperative is the takeaway — give the reader something they can carry out the door.

## What NOT to borrow

- **The numbered-footnote escape hatch.** PG drops [1] [2] [3] inside almost every paragraph to defer caveats. It breaks reading flow and signals he isn't quite willing to commit to what he just wrote. Either commit in the body or cut it.
- **The "Thanks to Sam Altman, Patrick Collison, Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris..." boilerplate close.** Mechanical and slightly clubby. If you want to thank readers, do it once at the bottom of a piece — not as ritual.
- **The reflexive YC-as-universal-evidence move.** "I've now worked with over 200 of them..." is fine the first time. Used as a credentialing tic, it narrows the audience to people who care about YC.
- **The mild "fuck" / "ass" / "lame" injected for hacker-tribe signaling.** Works once for emphasis; deployed as in-group register it dates the prose to a specific 2010 vibe.
- **Compulsive "in fact" / "actually" / "empirically" insertions.** Gestures at evidence without supplying it. Cut all but the one or two where the evidence is actually arriving.

## Calibration anchors

### Anchor 1: thesis-first opener defining the term

> A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary for a startup to work on technology, or take venture funding, or have some sort of "exit." The only essential thing is growth.

### Anchor 2: physical-domain analogy carrying the argument

> When people hurt themselves lifting heavy things, it's usually because they try to lift with their back. The right way to lift heavy things is to let your legs do the work. Inexperienced founders make the same mistake when trying to convince investors. They try to convince with their pitch.

### Anchor 3: math-as-proof demonstration

> Suppose your company is making $1000 a month now, and you've made something so great that it's growing at 5% a week. Two years from now, you'll be making about $160k a month. Now suppose you're so un-rapacious that you only extract half as much from your users as you could. That means two years later you'll be making $80k a month instead of $160k. How far behind are you? How long will it take to catch up with where you'd have been if you were extracting every penny? A mere 15 weeks.

### Anchor 4: coined term introduced and immediately put to work

> I'm going to call the situation I described in the first paragraph "the fatal pinch." I try to resist coining phrases, but making up a name for this situation may snap founders into realizing when they're in it.

### Anchor 5: way-to-X-is-not-to-Y inversion as the whole thesis

> The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself. The very best startup ideas tend to have three things in common: they're something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing.

### Anchor 6: two-word imperative as closer

> Be relentlessly resourceful.
