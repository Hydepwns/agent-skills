# Paul Graham — early era (2001-2004)

Source: https://www.paulgraham.com (essays dated 2001-04 through 2004-11, plus the pre-2001 *Programming Bottom-Up*)
Studied from: all 35 essays from this period — including "Beating the Averages", "How to Make Wealth", "Hackers and Painters", "What You Can't Say", "Great Hackers", "Mind the Gap", "The Age of the Essay", "Why Nerds are Unpopular", "The Python Paradox", "Revenge of the Nerds", "Taste for Makers", "A Plan for Spam", "The Hundred-Year Language", "Made in USA", and 21 others.

## The core move

Early-era PG argues by physical demonstration. Every abstract claim is weight-tested with a concrete scene — an Italian friend on a train, a desert island, a nimble guy chased into a staircase — and then closed with a two- or three-word kicker that turns the demonstration into a verdict. The prose feels like he's showing you something rather than telling you about it.

## Sentence rhythm

- Long expository build, then a short flat kicker. "For centuries the Japanese have made finer things than we have in the West. When you look at swords they made in 1200, you just can't believe the date on the label is right. They're obsessed with making things well. Not us." [from usa.txt]
- Conversational sentence-starters: "Well," "Indeed," "Actually," "Of course," "And" lead sentences and create a thinking-out-loud cadence. "Well, of course people want the wrong things. It seems odd to be surprised by that." [from gap.txt]
- Paired contrast clauses with mirrored grammar, often as paragraph engines. "Design doesn't have to be new, but it has to be good. Research doesn't have to be good, but it has to be new." [from desres.txt]
- Aphoristic mid-paragraph one-liners that read like detached maxims. "Argue with idiots, and you become an idiot." [from say.txt]. "Working on nasty little problems makes you stupid." [from gh.txt]
- One-word imperatives and questions dropped into expository prose as rhythm resets. "Race you." [from usa.txt]. "Why?" [from charisma.txt, taste.txt, gap.txt, repeatedly].

## Paragraph structure

- Anecdotal opener that names a specific person, year, or scene — not a thesis. "A few years ago an Italian friend of mine travelled by train from Boston to Providence." [from usa.txt]. "When we were in junior high school, my friend Rich and I made a map of the school lunch tables according to popularity." [from nerds.txt]
- One-sentence paragraphs used as hinges. "Not us." [from usa.txt]. "I wish." [from nerds.txt]. "Smack!" [from taste.txt].
- High length variance — 4-6 sentence developed paragraphs alternating with 1-2 sentence interjections.
- Closings reach outward to a maxim or imperative, never a recap. "Race you." [from usa.txt]. "Let the nerds keep their lunch money, and you rule the world." [from wealth.txt].
- Transitions via direct questions the reader would ask next. "How did things get this way?" [from essay.txt, laundry.txt]. "What happened?" [from polls.txt].

## Rhetorical moves

1. **Counterintuitive concession opening**: concede the obvious objection up front, then pivot to the sharper claim. "I didn't mean by this that Java programmers are dumb. I meant that Python programmers are smart." [from pypar.txt]
2. **Extended physical metaphor as load-bearing argument**: name a concrete physical scenario and run the argument off it. The staircase chase [from wealth.txt]. The big-company-as-galley [from wealth.txt]. The sword and the joinery [from usa.txt].
3. **Dropped one-line paragraph as punchline**: after a setup paragraph, isolate the kicker on its own line. "Not us." [from usa.txt]. "I wish." [from nerds.txt].
4. **Rhetorical question, answered immediately**: ask the reader's question and answer in the next breath. "How can you get errors asking that? Because some people don't respond." [from polls.txt]
5. **Named-law coinage**: christen a phenomenon with a phrase ("the Python Paradox," "the Daddy Model," "the Pie Fallacy"), then use the label as shorthand. "Hence what, for lack of a better name, I'll call the Python paradox..." [from pypar.txt]
6. **Historical zoom-out**: when discussing a present-day topic, abruptly reach back centuries for the cause. "How did things get this way? To answer that we have to go back almost a thousand years. Between about 500 and 1000, life was not very good in Europe." [from laundry.txt]
7. **Stripped-to-axiom thought experiment**: reduce a contested situation to one farmer on an island, or three nomads with sticks, to force the underlying logic. "To help clarify the matter, get rid of everyone else and put our worker on a desert island, hunting and gathering fruit." [from gap.txt]

## Emotional register

- Combative but playful. He's defending a tribe (hackers, makers, Lisp programmers) against mainstream dismissal, and the prose has visible adversarial energy without losing humor.
- Self-effacing parentheticals undercut the high register at exactly the right moment. "(I cheat by using a very dense language, which shrinks the court.)" [from gh.txt]
- Strong claims are common, but he repeatedly stages a "well, look at it this way" moment of physical re-grounding before committing — the staircase, the island, the sword. The reader trusts the conclusion because they watched the demonstration.
- Curiosity is the dominant note. Even essays that are technically polemics ("What You Can't Say") are framed as the writer working out the puzzle in front of you, not winning an argument.

## What to borrow

1. **Earn an abstract claim by running it through a thought experiment.** When you need to argue something contested, strip the situation down to two people on an island or three nomads collecting sticks. The reduction forces clarity and makes the conclusion feel inevitable instead of asserted.
2. **End a long paragraph on a two-word kicker.** Build the case across four or five sentences, then drop a flat declarative ("Not us." "I wish.") on its own line. The kicker compresses the paragraph into something portable.
3. **Open with a specific scene, not a thesis.** Name the person, year, or place. "A few years ago an Italian friend of mine travelled by train..." beats any topic sentence at making the reader stay.
4. **Coin a term mid-essay and use it as shorthand.** Name the phenomenon you're about to discuss ("the X paradox", "the Y effect") — the label compresses three paragraphs of argument into one phrase you can deploy again.
5. **Vary paragraph length dramatically.** A six-sentence build followed by a one-sentence paragraph followed by another six-sentence build. The rhythm signals a person thinking rather than an information delivery system.

## What NOT to borrow

- **The dismissive snide analogy.** "Computer science is a grab bag of tenuously related areas thrown together by an accident of history, like Yugoslavia." [from hp.txt] Works once; he uses it many times per essay and it starts to feel like an in-group sneer.
- **Over-reliance on "I think" / "I suspect" / "I have a hunch".** PG hedges almost every claim with one of these. In technical or product writing it dissolves the confidence the rest of the sentence is asking for. Drop the hedge when you actually know.
- **The reflexive "Of course" as filler.** It signals he's about to walk back the previous sentence. Use it once per piece, never as a tic.
- **Shared cultural assumptions about your audience.** Early PG is writing to STEM-educated readers who already know who Larry Wall is and what Lisp does. References that don't land universally read as gatekeeping.

## Calibration anchors

### Anchor 1: anecdotal opener that earns the abstract claim

> A few years ago an Italian friend of mine travelled by train from Boston to Providence. She had only been in America for a couple weeks and hadn't seen much of the country yet. She arrived looking astonished. "It's so ugly!"

### Anchor 2: long build closed by a flat two-word kicker

> For centuries the Japanese have made finer things than we have in the West. When you look at swords they made in 1200, you just can't believe the date on the label is right. Presumably their cars fit together more precisely than ours for the same reason their joinery always has. They're obsessed with making things well.
>
> Not us.

### Anchor 3: paired contrast clauses with mirrored grammar

> Design doesn't have to be new, but it has to be good. Research doesn't have to be good, but it has to be new. I think these two paths converge at the top: the best design surpasses its predecessors by using new ideas, and the best research solves problems that are not only new, but actually worth solving.

### Anchor 4: extended physical metaphor as the whole argument

> Suppose you are a little, nimble guy being chased by a big, fat, bully. You open a door and find yourself in a staircase. Do you go up or down? I say up. The bully can probably run downstairs as fast as you can. Going upstairs his bulk will be more of a disadvantage.

### Anchor 5: thought-experiment reduction to force the underlying logic

> To help clarify the matter, get rid of everyone else and put our worker on a desert island, hunting and gathering fruit. If he's bad at it he'll work very hard and not end up with much food. Is this unfair? Who is being unfair to him?
