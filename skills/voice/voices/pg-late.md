# Paul Graham — late era (2016-present)

Source: https://www.paulgraham.com (essays dated 2016-01 through 2026, the post-YC-active period)
Studied from: all 60 essays from this period — including "How to Do Great Work", "The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius", "How to Think for Yourself", "The Four Quadrants of Conformism", "Earnestness", "Putting Ideas into Words", "Write Simply", "The Need to Read", "The Right Kind of Stubborn", "Founder Mode", "The Origins of Wokeness", "Writes and Write-Nots", "Superlinear Returns", "Heresy", "How to Work Hard", "A Project of One's Own", "Beyond Smart", "Life is Short", "The Refragmentation", "Is There Such a Thing as Good Taste?", "The Lesson to Unlearn", "Haters", and 38 others.

## The core move

Late-era PG thinks on the page in front of you. Instead of arriving with a thesis and defending it, he stages the refinement: he states a position, names what's wrong with it, restates, refines again, and lets you watch the conclusion get sharpened. The prose openly acknowledges that the essay *is* the thinking — "In fact I only really figured it out as I was writing this essay" appears as itself.

## Sentence rhythm

- Short declarative followed by a longer elaborating sentence. "Obstinacy is a simple thing. Animals have it. But persistence turns out to have a fairly complicated internal structure." [from persistence.txt]
- Three-beat declaratives used as section landings. "Speed defines startups. Focus enables speed. YC improves focus." [from users.txt]
- Em-dash refinements inserted mid-sentence to narrow a claim without breaking cadence. "You could be a horrible person, and yet as long as you're orthodox, you're better than everyone who isn't." [from heresy.txt]
- Auto-corrective second sentence: a claim is made, the next sentence narrows it with "Or more precisely..." or "A more precise version would be...". "More precisely, a high ratio of size to accuracy meant cheap." [from brandage.txt]
- Standalone questions used as gear shifts between paragraphs. "Why? Why has this antiquated-sounding religious concept come back in a secular form? And why now?" [from heresy.txt]

## Paragraph structure

- Topic sentence first, then one supporting example, then a generalization. Claim → instance → zoom-out, in three to six sentences.
- Short paragraphs (two to four sentences) used as load-bearing turns. Single-sentence paragraphs are deployed as drumbeats. "Branding is centrifugal; design is centripetal." [from brandage.txt] sits alone.
- Recursive self-correction across consecutive paragraphs. A paragraph asserts X; the next opens "That's still not quite true" or "In fact this is an illusion" and refines. The reader watches the position evolve.
- Paragraphs built as deductive chains. "So... But... Which means... So..." stretched across the sentences within one paragraph, producing visible inference.
- Footnotes are even heavier than in the startup era — 16 to 29 numbered notes per long essay. Qualifications, exceptions, and second-order asides all live downstairs so the body cadence stays clean.

## Rhetorical moves

1. **Definition-by-contrast (anchor pair)**: define a target by yoking it to its near-twin and separating them with twin sentences in balanced grammar. "The persistent are attached to the goal. The obstinate are attached to their ideas about how to reach it." [from persistence.txt]. "There are two distinct ways to be politically moderate: on purpose and by accident." [from mod.txt]
2. **Reductio chain via "which means... which means..."**: march a premise through three to five implications until it reaches an absurd or revelatory consequence. "So if you want to discard the concept of good taste, you also have to discard the concept of good art. And that means you have to discard the possibility of people being good at making it. Which means there's no way for artists to be good at their jobs." [from goodtaste.txt]
3. **Recursive self-correction**: state a formulation, name what's wrong with it, refine. "So we modify our initial statement: You can do what you want, but you can't want to want what you want. That's still not quite true. It's possible to change what you want to want... But we're getting closer to the truth." [from want.txt]
4. **Naming the move explicitly**: coin a portable label mid-essay with "I call this X." "I call this 'staying upwind.'" [from when.txt, greatwork.txt]. "This is an instance of what I call the comb-over effect: when a series of individually small changes takes you from something that's a little bit off to something that's freakishly wrong." [from brandage.txt]
5. **Algorithmic / CS framing of a fuzzy phenomenon**: import depth-first search, O(n²), Riemann sums, the sign bit, to formalize an intuition. "Doing great work is a depth-first search whose root node is the desire to. So 'If at first you don't succeed, try, try again' isn't quite right. It should be: If at first you don't succeed, either try again, or backtrack and then try again." [from greatwork.txt]
6. **Live-thinking meta-commentary**: explicitly note that the essay is the discovery. "In fact I only really figured it out as I was writing this essay." [from field.txt]. "Bizarrely enough, this happened in the writing of this very paragraph." [from goodwriting.txt]
7. **Identity-reveal punchline**: describe two phenomena in parallel paragraphs, then collapse them. "I spent years puzzling about haters. What are they, and where do they come from? Then one day it dawned on me. Haters are just fanboys with the sign switched." [from fh.txt]
8. **Aphorism-as-paragraph-payload**: a section's argument collapses into one declarative line, balanced or triadic, and the next paragraph moves on. "The number of true things we can't say should not increase. If it does, something is wrong." [from woke.txt]

## Emotional register

- More confident and more controversial than earlier eras. Late PG knows he is going to upset someone with each essay and frames the upset as evidence the topic was worth writing about.
- The recursive refinement signals openness — the writer demonstrably changed his mind across the page — but the final position lands firmly.
- Confessional asides have grown into structural elements rather than parentheticals. "I'm biased, I admit, but..." opens whole paragraphs.
- The voice has slowed. Sentences breathe more. Aphorisms get more space. Where startup-era PG was clipped and instructional, late PG is willing to sit with an idea for two or three paragraphs before naming it.

## What to borrow

1. **Pair sentences in twin/contrast form.** "The persistent are attached to the goal. The obstinate are attached to their ideas about how to reach it." Two sentences with mirrored grammar do the work of an entire definition section. Use this when distinguishing close concepts.
2. **Show your refinement.** When you state a position, then state the better version, then state the still-better version, the final formulation feels earned instead of declared. "That's still not quite true. It's possible to..." is the load-bearing phrase.
3. **Run a reductio chain.** If you suspect a popular claim has hidden absurd consequences, walk it forward with "which means..." three or four times. The chain does the persuading; you don't need to argue.
4. **Name the move you're making.** "I call this X." Coining a label crystallizes a behavior into a usable handle. Don't be precious about the name — pick a short physical phrase ("staying upwind," "the comb-over effect").
5. **Use live-thinking meta-commentary sparingly.** Once per essay, acknowledge that the essay is itself the discovery. "I only really figured this out as I was writing it." It signals honest cognition rather than rhetorical performance.

## What NOT to borrow

- **The "Obviously" / "Of course" softener for premises that aren't obvious.** Late PG asserts the controversial as if uncontroversial and waves "obviously" at it. This is rhetorical foot-fastening; the reader can feel the move.
- **The "in fact" / "indeed" pivot used dozens of times per essay.** At low frequency it intensifies; at high frequency it becomes a verbal fingerprint that gets in the way of the argument.
- **The "I'm not going to chase down all the implications now" exit ramp.** Drops a heavy claim and then explicitly declines to defend it. If you can't defend it, don't drop it. If you can, do it.
- **Self-referential "as I've written elsewhere"**, treating the whole corpus as a single growing argument. Each essay should stand alone.
- **Reflexive YC / Silicon Valley reference as universal evidence.** A Bay Area startup observation generalized to humanity often doesn't survive the generalization.
